05.Example 2

Given:

A composite wall separates combustion gases at $2600 ^\circ C$ from a liquid coolant at $100 ^\circ C$, with gas and liquid side convection coefficients of $50$ and $1000 \frac{W}{m^2K}$, respectively. The wall is composed of $10 mm$ thick layer of beryllium oxide on the gas side and a $20 mm$ thick slab of stainless steel (AISI 304) on the liquid side. The contact resistance between the oxide and the steel is $0.05 \frac{m^2K}{W}$.

Required:

What is the heat lost per unit surface area of the composite? Sketch the temperature distribution from the gas to the liquid.

Solution:

Start first by importing any needed libraries.

In[1]:
import pint # https://pint.readthedocs.io

The unit registry may then be defined. This is shown below.

In[2]:
ureg = pint.UnitRegistry()
Q_ = ureg.Quantity

The given problem variables may now be defined with units.

In[3]:
T_inf1 = Q_(2600, 'degC')
T_inf2 = Q_(100, 'degC')
h_1 = Q_(50, 'W/(m**2*K)')
h_2 = Q_(1000, 'W/(m**2*K)')
L_A = Q_(10, 'mm')
L_B = Q_(20, 'mm')
R_tc_flux = Q_(0.05, '(m**2*K)/W')

A drawing of the system is shown below.

Section A and B are the beryllium oxide and stainless steel, respectively. Their material properties may be found from the Appendix. However, evaluating material properties at a given temperature is problematic because the two materials are not at a constant temperature and not all temperature points are known (e.g. the temperature on either side of the material interface). For the most accurate solution, an iterative approach must be used. However, this can be quite time consuming and tedious. For the solution presented below, a guess will be made for the temperatures at which the material property will be evaluated. The guess value here will be the average of the two extreme temperatures
$\bar{T}=\frac{T_{\infty, 1}+T_{\infty, 2}}{2}$

In[4]:
Tbar = (T_inf1.to('K') + T_inf2.to('K'))/2
Tbar
Out[4]:
1623.15 kelvin

From Table A.2 for beryllium oxide at $\bar{T}$ shows that interpolation is needed for the thermal conductivity. This is shown below.

In[5]:
T_1 = Q_(1500, 'K')
T_2 = Q_(2000, 'K')
k_1 = Q_(21.5, 'W/(m*K)')
k_2 = Q_(15, 'W/(m*K)')
k_A = ((Tbar - T_1)/(T_2 - T_1))*(k_2 - k_1) + k_1
round(k_A, 2)
Out[5]:
19.9 watt/(kelvin meter)

For stainless steel (AISI 304), Table A.1 only goes up to $T=1500K$ so this value will be used. This is shown below.

In[6]:
k_B = Q_(31.7, 'W/(m*K)')

A thermal circuit may be drawn to represent the system. This is shown below.

From the thermal circuit it may be seen that the total thermal resistance is
$R^{''}_{tot}=\frac{1}{h_1}+\frac{L_A}{k_A}+R^{''}_{t,c}+\frac{L_B}{k_B}+\frac{1}{h_2}$

In[7]:
R_tot_flux = 1/h_1 + L_A/k_A + R_tc_flux + L_B/k_B + 1/h_2
round(R_tot_flux, 5)
Out[7]:
0.07213 kelvin meter2/watt

The heat flux may then be found by
$q^{''}=\frac{T_{\infty,1}-T_{\infty,2}}{R^{''}_{tot}}$

In[8]:
q_flux =(T_inf1 - T_inf2)/R_tot_flux
round(q_flux.to('kW/m**2'), 2)    # answer to first part
Out[8]:
34.66 kilowatt/meter2

To determine the temperature distribution, the temperature at each node must be determined. This is shown below. $T_1$ may be found by
$T_1=T_{\infty,1}-q^{''}\frac{1}{h_1}$

In[9]:
T_1 = T_inf1 - q_flux/h_1
round(T_1, 1)
Out[9]:
1906.8 degree_Celsius

$T_{iA}$ may be found by
$T_{iA}=T_1-q^{''}\frac{L_A}{k_A}$

In[10]:
T_iA = T_1 - q_flux*L_A/k_A
round(T_iA, 1)
Out[10]:
1889.4 degree_Celsius

$T_{iB}$ may be found by
$T_{iB}=T_{iA}-q^{''}R^{''}_{t,c}$

In[11]:
T_iB = T_iA - q_flux*R_tc_flux
round(T_iB, 1)
Out[11]:
156.5 degree_Celsius

$T_2$ may be found by
$T_2=T_{iB}-q^{''}\frac{L_B}{k_B}$

In[12]:
T_2 = T_iB - q_flux*L_B/k_B
round(T_2, 1)
Out[12]:
134.7 degree_Celsius

Now that all the temperature nodes are determined, the temperature distribution may be sketched. This is shown below.

At this point if an iterative method was being used the material properties at their average temperatures would be reevalulated and the steps taken to find the quantities of interest would be repeated. If the values of the quantities of interest remain the same, a solution is reached. However, for a quick estimate the iterative method is not being used and the values reported above will be considered close enough.
