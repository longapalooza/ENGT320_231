10.Example 1¶

Given:¶

A system for heating water from an inlet temperature of $T_{m,i} = 20 ^\circ C$ to an outlet temperature of $T_{m,o} = 60 ^\circ C$ involves passing the water through a thick walled tube having inner and outer diameters of $20$ and $40 mm$. The outer surface of the tube is well insulated, and electrical heating within the wall provides for a uniform generation rate of $q = 10^6 \frac{W}{m^3}$.

Required:¶

  1. For a water mass flow rate of $m = 0.1 \frac{kg}{s}$, how long must the tube be to achieve the desired outlet temperature?
  2. If the inner surface temperature of the tube is $T_s = 70 ^\circ C$ at the outlet, what is the local convection heat transfer coefficient at the outlet?

Solution:¶

Start first by importing any needed libraries.

In [1]:
import math
import pint # https://pint.readthedocs.io

The unit registry may then be defined. This is shown below.

In [2]:
ureg=pint.UnitRegistry()
Q_=ureg.Quantity

The given problem variables may now be defined with units.

In [3]:
T_mi=Q_(20, 'degC')
T_mo=Q_(60, 'degC')
D_i=Q_(20, 'mm')
D_o=Q_(40, 'mm')
q=Q_(1E6, 'W/m**3')
m=Q_(0.1, 'kg/s')
T_so=Q_(70, 'degC')

A diagram of the system is shown below.

The fluid properties will be evaluated at the average mean temperature. This is given by
    $\bar{T}_m=\frac{T_{m,o}+T_{m,i}}{2}$

In [4]:
Tb_m=(T_mo.to('K')+T_mi.to('K'))/2
round(Tb_m.to('K'), 1)
Out[4]:
313.1 kelvin

Going to Table A.6 at $\bar{T}_m$ shows that interpolation is needed. This is shown below.

In [5]:
Ta=Q_(310, 'K')
Tb=Q_(315, 'K')
c_pa=Q_(4.178, 'kJ/(kg*K)')
c_pb=Q_(4.179, 'kJ/(kg*K)')
c_p=((Tb_m-Ta)/(Tb-Ta))*(c_pb-c_pa)+c_pa
round(c_p, 3)
Out[5]:
4.179 kilojoule/(kelvin kilogram)

Starting with an energy balance around the volume of the pipe shows
    $\frac{dE_{sys}}{dt}=\Sigma \dot{E}_{in} - \Sigma \dot{E}_{out}$
Assuming steady state conditions shows
    $0=\Sigma \dot{E}_{in} - \Sigma \dot{E}_{out}$
The energy entering the pipe is through the heat generation term and is given by
    $\Sigma \dot{E}_{in} = q V = q \frac{\pi}{4}\left( D_o^2 - D_i^2 \right)L$
The energy leaving the pipe is due to the convection heat rate to the fluid and as previous shown in the lecture notes is given by
    $\Sigma \dot{E}_{out} = q_{conv} = m c_p \left( T_{m,o} - T_{m,i} \right)$
The energy balance then becomes
    $0=q \frac{\pi}{4}\left( D_o^2 - D_i^2 \right)L - m c_p \left( T_{m,o} - T_{m,i} \right)$
Solving for the length $L$ of the pipe shows
    $L=\frac{4 m c_p \left( T_{m,o} - T_{m,i} \right)}{\pi q \left( D_o^2 - D_i^2 \right)}$

In [6]:
L=(4*m*c_p*(T_mo-T_mi))/(math.pi*q*(D_o**2-D_i**2))
round(L.to('m'), 2)    # answer to part 1
Out[6]:
17.73 meter

Assuming that the heat generation term produces a constant surface heat flux condition on the pipe, the surface heat flux may be found
    $q^{''}_s=\frac{qV}{A_s}=\frac{q\frac{\pi}{4}\left( D_o^2 - D_i^2 \right)L}{\pi D_i L}=\frac{q\left( D_o^2 - D_i^2 \right)}{4 D_i}$

In [7]:
qf_s=(q*(D_o**2-D_i**2))/(4*D_i)
round(qf_s.to('kW/m**2'), 2)
Out[7]:
15.0 kilowatt/meter2

Using Newton's law of cooling the convective coefficient at the outlet may be found. This is shown below.
    $q^{''}_s=h_o \left( T_{s,o} - T_{m,o} \right)$
Solving for the convective coefficient shows
    $h_o=\frac{q^{''}_s}{T_{s,o}-T_{m,o}}$

In [8]:
h_o=qf_s/(T_so-T_mo)
round(h_o.to('kW/(m**2*K)'), 3)    # answer to part 2
Out[8]:
1.5 kilowatt/(kelvin meter2)
