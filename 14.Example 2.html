14.Example 2

Given:

The cover glass on a flat-plate solar collector has a low iron content, and its spectral transmissivity may be approximated by the following distribution.

Required:

What is the total transmissivity of the cover glass to solar radiation?

Solution:

Start first by importing any needed libraries.

In[1]:
import math
import pint # https://pint.readthedocs.io #

The unit registry may then be defined. This is shown below.

In[2]:
ureg=pint.UnitRegistry()
Q_=ureg.Quantity

The given problem variables may now be defined with units.

In[3]:
tau_1 = 0.9
lambda_1 = Q_(0.3, 'um')
lambda_2 = Q_(2.5,'um')

The total transmissivity of the cover is given by $$ \tau = \frac{\int_0^\infty \tau_\lambda G_\lambda d \lambda}{\int_0^\infty G_\lambda d\lambda} $$

where the irradiation $ G_\lambda $ is due to solar emission. Having assumed that the sun emits as a blackbody at $ 5800 K $, it follows that $$ G_\lambda \left( \lambda \right) \propto E_{\lambda, b} \left( 5800 K \right) $$

The total transmissivity may then be written as $$ \tau = \frac{\int_0^\infty \tau_\lambda E_{\lambda, b} \left( 5800 K \right) d \lambda}{\int_0^\infty E_{\lambda, b} \left( 5800 K \right) d\lambda} $$

The expression above may be further simplified by first recognizing that the denominator is the emissive power of a blackbody at $ 5800 K $. $$ \tau = \frac{\int_0^\infty \tau_\lambda E_{\lambda, b} \left( 5800 K \right) d \lambda}{E_b \left( 5800 K \right)} $$

The second step to simplify the expression above is to split the numerator's integral into different regions. $$ \tau = \frac{\int_0^{\lambda_1} \tau_0 E_{\lambda, b} \left( 5800 K \right) d \lambda}{E_b \left( 5800 K \right)} + \frac{\int_{\lambda_1}^{\lambda_2} \tau_1 E_{\lambda, b} \left( 5800 K \right) d \lambda}{E_b \left( 5800 K \right)} + \frac{\int_{\lambda_2}^\infty \tau_0 E_{\lambda, b} \left( 5800 K \right) d \lambda}{E_b \left( 5800 K \right)} $$

Using the plot given in the problem statement, it may be realized that the first and last region go to zero. The simplified expression then becomes $$ \tau = \frac{\int_{\lambda_1}^{\lambda_2} \tau_1 E_{\lambda, b} \left( 5800 K \right) d \lambda}{E_b \left( 5800 K \right)} $$

In the interval that is left, the transmissivity is constant so it may be taken out of the integral. $$ \tau = \tau_1 \frac{\int_{\lambda_1}^{\lambda_2} E_{\lambda, b} \left( 5800 K \right) d \lambda}{E_b \left( 5800 K \right)} $$

Once the transmissivity has been taken out of the integral, it may be recognized that the remaining fraction is the $ F $ from Table 8 of the Quiz 5 Formula Sheet. This is written as $$ \tau = \tau_1 F_{\lambda_1 \rightarrow \lambda_2} = \tau_1 \left( F_{0 \rightarrow \lambda_2} - F_{0 \rightarrow \lambda_1} \right) $$

To use Table 8, the product of the wavelength and temperature is used.

In[4]:
T = Q_(5800, 'K')
lamT_1 = lambda_1 * T
print(lamT_1)

1740.0 kelvin * micrometer
In[5]:
lamT_2 = lambda_2 * T
print(lamT_2)

14500.0 kelvin * micrometer

Referring to Table 8, it may be seen that interpolation is needed.

In[6]:
lamT_a = Q_(1600, 'um*K')
lamT_b = Q_(1800, 'um*K')
F_a = 0.019719
F_b = 0.039342
F_0lam1 = ((lamT_1 - lamT_a)/(lamT_b - lamT_a))*(F_b - F_a) + F_a
print(F_0lam1)

0.0334551 dimensionless
In[7]:
lamT_a = Q_(10000, 'um*K')
lamT_b = Q_(20000, 'um*K')
F_a = 0.914157
F_b = 0.985554
F_0lam2 = ((lamT_2 - lamT_a)/(lamT_b - lamT_a))*(F_b - F_a) + F_a
print(F_0lam2)

0.94628565 dimensionless

The total transmissivity is then

In[8]:
tau = tau_1*(F_0lam2 - F_0lam1)
print(tau)    # answer #

0.821547495 dimensionless
