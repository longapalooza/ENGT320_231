15.Example 3

Given:

Consider an air heater consisting of a semicircular tube for which the plane surface is maintained at $ 1000 K $ and the other surface is well insulated. The tube radius is $ 20 mm $, and both surfaces have an emissivity of $ 0.8 $.

Required:

If atmospheric air flows through the tube at $ 0.01 \frac{kg}{s} $ and $ T_m = 400 K $, what is the temperature of the insulated surface?

Solution:

Start first by importing any needed libraries.

In[1]:
import math
import pint # https://pint.readthedocs.io

The unit registry may then be defined. This is shown below.

In[2]:
ureg = pint.UnitRegistry()
Q_ = ureg.Quantity

The given problem variables may now be defined with units.

In[3]:
T_1 = Q_(1000, 'K')

eps = Q_(0.8, '')
mdot = Q_(0.01, 'kg/s')
T_m = Q_(400, 'K')

Other known quantities that will be used are shown below.

In[4]:
sigma = Q_(5.67*10**-8, 'W/(m^2*K^4)')    # Stefan-Boltzman constant #

A sketch of the system is shown below.

The equivalent radiation circuit is shown below.

Beginning with a 1st Law look of the surface of the semicircular portion yields $$ \frac{dE_{sys}}{dt} = \Sigma E_{in} - \Sigma E_{out} $$ $$ \frac{dE_{sys}}{dt} = - q_{2,rad} - q_{2,conv} $$ For steady state conditions, the surface energy balance becomes $$ 0 = - q_{2,rad} - q_{2,conv} $$ Thus, $$ - q_{2,rad} = q_{2,conv} $$ or $$ \frac{\sigma \left(T_1^4 - T_2^4 \right)}{R_{tot}} = h A_2 \left(T_2 - T_m \right) $$

The equivalent resistance of the radiation circuit is $$ R_{tot} = \frac{1-\varepsilon}{\varepsilon A_1} + \frac{1}{A_1 F_{12}} + \frac{1-\varepsilon}{\varepsilon A_2} $$

The surface areas per unit length are given by $$ A_1 = 2 r_o $$ $$ A_2 = \pi r_o $$

In[5]:
A_1 = 2*r_o

To find the convective coefficient $ h $, the hydraulic diameter must first be found. The hydraulic diameter is given by $$ D_h = \frac{4 A_c}{P} $$ where $ A_c $ is the cross-sectional area, and $ P $ is the wetted perimeter. The cross-sectional area is given by $$ A_c = \frac{\pi r_o^2}{2} $$

To calculate the Reynolds, material properties of air must first be found. Going to Table A.4 at $ T_m $ shows

In[12]:

mu = Q_(230.1E-7, 'N*s/m**2')
k = Q_(33.8E-3, 'W/(m*K)')
Pr = 0.690

The Reynolds number may now be calculated by $$ Re_D = \frac{\rho u_m D_h}{\mu} $$ Recognizing that mass flow rate is $$ \dot{m} = \rho u_m A_c $$ The Reynolds number expression becomes $$ Re_D = \frac{\dot{m} D_h}{A_c \mu} $$

In[13]:
Re_D = mdot*D_h/(A_c*mu)
Re_D.to('')
Out[13]:
16905.02084521263 dimensionless

The Reynolds number is significantly high so fully developed turbulent flow will be assumed. Referring to the reference equations for internal flow for various geometries shows that a semicircular cross-section is not one of the listed geometries. In light of this, a circular cross-section will be assumed. Thus, the Nusselt number is given by $$ Nu_D = 0.023 Re_D^{\frac{4}{5}} Pr^{0.4} $$

In[14]:
Nu_D = 0.023*Re_D**(4/5)*Pr**0.4
Nu_D.to('')
Out[14]:
47.82775994757079 dimensionless

The convective coefficient is then given by $$ h = \frac{k Nu_D}{D_h} $$

In[15]:
h = k*Nu_D.to('')/D_h
h.to('W/(m**2*K)')
Out[15]:
66.1430996705167 watt/(kelvin meter2)

Now that the convective coefficient has been found, the only unknown variable is $ T_2 $. Unfortunately, $ T_2 $ may not be found explicitly. An iterative approach must be used to find $ T_2 $. The first step is to solve the previously found surface energy balance for a single $ T_2 $. This is shown below. $$ \frac{\sigma \left(T_1^4 - T_2^4 \right)}{R_{tot}} = h A_2 \left(T_2 - T_m \right) $$ $$ T_2 = \frac{\frac{\sigma \left(T_1^4 - T_2^4 \right)}{R_{tot}} + h A_2 T_m}{h A_2} $$

The next step is to make an initial guess for $ T_2 $. A maximum value that could be expected from $ T_2 $ is $ 1000 K $. This will be used for the initial guess.

In[16]:
T_2 = Q_(1000, 'K')

Now that a guess has been made, the value of $ T_2 $ may be calculated. This is shown below.

In[17]:
T_2 = (sigma*(T_1**4 - T_2**4)/R_tot + h*A_2*T_m)/(h*A_2)
