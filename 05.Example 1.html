05.Example 1¶

Given:¶

Consider a person that has a layer of skin/fat covered with a special sporting gear suit, with the outer surface exposed to air at a temperature of $10^\circ C$ with a convective heat transfer coefficient of $2 \frac{W}{m^2K}$. The inner surface (under the layer of skin/fat) is maintained at a constant temperature of $35 ^\circ C$ through a process called thermoregulation. The skin/fat layer has a thickness of $3 mm$ and an effective thermal conductivity of $0.3 \frac{W}{mK}$. The total surface area of the skin/fat is $1.8 m^2$. The special sporting gear suit has a thermal conductivity of $0.014 \frac{W}{mK}$. The emissivity of the outer surface of the special sporting gear suit is $0.95$.

Required:¶

What thickness of the sporting gear suit is needed to reduce the heat loss rate to $100 W$? What is the resulting skin temperature?

Solution:¶

Start first by importing any needed libraries.

import pint # https://pint.readthedocs.io

The unit registry may then be defined. This is shown below.

In [2]:
ureg = pint.UnitRegistry()
Q_ = ureg.Quantity

The given problem variables may now be defined with units.

In [3]:
T_inf = Q_(10, 'degC')
h = Q_(2, 'W/(m**2*K)')
T_i = Q_(35, 'degC')
L_sf = Q_(3, 'mm')
k_sf = Q_(0.3, 'W/(m*K)')
A = Q_(1.8, 'm**2')
k_ins = Q_(0.014, 'W/(m*K)')
eps = 0.95
q = Q_(100, 'W')

Since an emissivity is given, there must be radiation occurring. The surrounding temperature may then be assumed the same as the air temperature. This is shown below.

In [4]:
T_sur = T_inf

Other known quantities that will be used are shown below.

In [5]:
sig = Q_(5.67*10**-8, 'W/(m^2*K^4)')    # Stefan-Boltzman constant

The drawing of the system is shown below.

A solution may be reached by drawing the thermal circuit of the system. This is shown below.

Looking at the circuit, it may be seen that the skin temperature may be found quite straightforwardly. Recognizing that the temperature drop across the skin is
    $T_i-T_s=q\frac{L_{sf}}{k_{sf}A}$
Solving for the skin temperature is then
    $T_s=T_i-q\frac{L_{sf}}{k_{sf}A}$

In [6]:
T_s = T_i - q*L_sf/(k_sf*A)
round(T_s.to('degC'), 1)    # answer to second part
Out[6]:
34.4 degree_Celsius

Looking at the circuit, it may be seen that the total thermal resistance may be found by
    $R_{tot}=\frac{T_i-T_{\infty}}{q}$

In [7]:
R_tot = (T_i - T_inf)/q
R_tot
Out[7]:
0.25 delta_degree_Celsius/watt

The total thermal resistance is also equal to
    $R_{tot}=\frac{L_{sf}}{k_{sf}A}+\frac{L_{ins}}{k_{ins}A}+\frac{1}{\frac{1}{\frac{1}{hA}}+\frac{1}{\frac{1}{h_rA}}}$
Rearranging shows
    $R_{tot}=\frac{1}{A}\left(\frac{L_{sf}}{k_{sf}}+\frac{L_{ins}}{k_{ins}}+\frac{1}{h+h_r}\right)$
Inspecting the expression, it may be seen that there are only two unknowns, $L_{ins}$ and $h_r$. Since $L_{ins}$ is what the problem is asking for, this is ok to be as an unknown. $h_r$ must first be determined to find $L_{ins}$. $h_r$ is defined as
    $h_r=\varepsilon\sigma\left(T_1+T_{sur}\right)\left(T_1^2+T_{sur}^2\right)$
where $T_1$ is the temperature at the exterior surface of the special sporting gear suit. Inspecting the expression above reveals that $T_1$ is unknown. To find $T_1$, $L_{ins}$ would need to be known. Since $L_{ins}$ is unknown, an impasse is reached. To solve this method, an iterative technique must be used. Iterative technique works by first making a guess for one uknown variable and then calculating some other variable with the guess. The calculated value is used to calculate the first guessed variable value. If the guessed value and the calculated value are the same, a solution is reach. If the two values are different, another iteration of guessing must be done. This is repeated until the two numbers are the same. The iterative technique is said to have converged at this point. To begin the iterative technique for this problem, an initial guess of $T_1$ is made.
Iteration 1

In [8]:
T_1 = (T_i.to('K') + T_inf.to('K'))/2
T_1.to('degC')
Out[8]:
22.5 degree_Celsius

An intelligent guess may be made for the first guess. It is reasonable to assume that $T_1$ will fall between $T_i$ and $T_{\infty}$ so the simple average of the two is used as the initial guess. Now the radiation heat transfer coefficient may be calculated.

In [9]:
h_r = eps*sig*(T_1.to('K') + T_sur.to('K'))*(T_1.to('K')**2 + T_sur.to('K')**2)
round(h_r, 2)
Out[9]:
5.22 watt/(kelvin meter2)

$L_{ins}$ may now be found by solving the total thermal resistance expression for it. This is shown below.     $L_{ins}=k_{ins}\left(A R_{tot}-\frac{L_{sf}}{k_{sf}}-\frac{1}{h+h_r}\right)$

In [10]:
L_ins = k_ins*(A*R_tot - L_sf/k_sf - 1/(h + h_r))
round(L_ins, 2)
Out[10]:
4.22 millimeter

Now comes the time to check the initial guess for $T_1$. This may be accomplished by recognizing the temperature drop across the special sporting gear suit to be
    $T_s-T_1=q\frac{L_{ins}}{k_{ins}A}$
Solving for $T_1$ shows
    $T_1=T_s-q\frac{L_{ins}}{k_{ins}A}$

In [11]:
T_1_check = T_s - q*L_ins/(k_ins*A)
round(T_1_check.to('degC'), 1)
Out[11]:
17.7 degree_Celsius

Since the temperature does not match the initial guess made for $T_1$ a solution has not been reached and another iteration must be completed. This is shown below.
Iteration 2
The second iteration starts by setting the last calculated value of $T_1$ to the guess for $T_1$.

In [12]:
T_1 = T_1_check

The $h_r$ is then calculated with this new value.

In [13]:
h_r = eps*sig*(T_1.to('K') + T_sur.to('K'))*(T_1.to('K')**2 + T_sur.to('K')**2)
round(h_r, 2)
Out[13]:
5.09 watt/(kelvin meter2)

Then the special sporting gear thickness is found with the updated value.

In [14]:
L_ins = k_ins*(A*R_tot - L_sf/k_sf - 1/(h + h_r))
round(L_ins, 2)
Out[14]:
4.19 millimeter

Finally the initial guess of $T_1$ is checked.

In [15]:
T_1_check = T_s - q*L_ins/(k_ins*A)
round(T_1_check.to('degC'), 1)
Out[15]:
17.8 degree_Celsius

As can be seen, the temperature on this iteration is very close the initial guess for the temperature. This means that the iterative technique is close to having converged. One more iteration should do the trick. This is shown below.
Iteration 3

In [16]:
T_1 = T_1_check
h_r = eps*sig*(T_1.to('K') + T_sur.to('K'))*(T_1.to('K')**2 + T_sur.to('K')**2)
round(h_r, 2)
Out[16]:
5.1 watt/(kelvin meter2)
