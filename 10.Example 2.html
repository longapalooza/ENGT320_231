10.Example 2

Given:

Steam condensing on the outer surface of a thin walled circular tube of diameter $D = 50 mm$ and length $L = 6 m$ maintains a uniform outer surface temperature of $100 ^\circ C$. Water flows through the tube at a rate of $m = 0.25 \frac{kg}{s}$, and its inlet and outlet temperatures are $T_{m,i} = 15 ^\circ C$ and $T_{m,o} = 57 ^\circ C$.

Required:

What is the average convection coefficient associated with the water flow?

Solution:

Start first by importing any needed libraries.

In[1]:
import math
import pint # https://pint.readthedocs.io

The unit registry may then be defined. This is shown below.

In[2]:
ureg=pint.UnitRegistry()
Q_=ureg.Quantity

The given problem variables may now be defined with units.

In[3]:
D=Q_(50, 'mm')
L=Q_(6, 'm')
T_s=Q_(100, 'degC')
m=Q_(0.25, 'kg/s')
T_mi=Q_(15, 'degC')
T_mo=Q_(57, 'degC')

A sketch of the system is shown below.

The fluid properties will be evaluated at the average mean temperature. This is given by
$\bar{T}_m = \frac{T_{m,i} + T_{m,o}}{2}$

In[4]:
Tb_m=(T_mi.to('K')+T_mo.to('K'))/2
round(Tb_m, 1)
Out[4]:
309.1 kelvin

Going to Table A.6 at $\bar{T}_m$ shows that the specific heat at constant pressure is

In[5]:
c_p=Q_(4.178, 'kJ/(kg*K)')

Beginning with a surface energy balance for the interface between the fluid and the inside of the pipe shows that for steady state conditions
$m c_p \Delta T_m=q_{conv}=\bar{h} A_s \Delta T_{lm}$
Solving for the convective coefficient shows
$\bar{h}=\frac{m c_p \Delta T_m}{A_s \Delta T_{lm}}$
The log mean temperature difference is given by
$\Delta T_{lm} = \frac{\left( T_s - T_{m,o} \right) - \left( T_s - T_{m,i} \right)}{\ln \left( \frac{T_s - T_{m,o}}{T_s - T_{m,i}} \right)}$

In[6]:
delT_lm=((T_s-T_mo) - (T_s-T_mi))/math.log((T_s-T_mo)/(T_s-T_mi))
round(delT_lm.to('K'), 2)
Out[6]:
61.63 kelvin

The surface area $A_s$ is given by
$A_s=\pi D L$

In[7]:
A_s=math.pi*D*L
round(A_s.to('m**2'), 4)
Out[7]:
0.9425 meter2

The convective coefficient is then given by
$\bar{h} = \frac{m c_p \left( T_{m,o} - T_{m,i} \right)}{A_s \Delta T_{lm}}$

In[8]:
hb=(m*c_p*(T_mo-T_mi))/(A_s*delT_lm)
round(hb.to('W/(m**2*K)'), 1)    # answer
Out[8]:
755.2 watt/(kelvin meter2)
