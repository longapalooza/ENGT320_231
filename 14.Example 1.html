14.Example 1

Given:

A diffuse surface at $ 1600 K $ has the spectral, hemispherical emissivity shown as follows.

Required:

Determine the total, hemispherical emissivity and the total emissive power.

Solution:

Start first by importing any needed libraries.

In[1]:
import math
import pint # https://pint.readthedocs.io #

The unit registry may then be defined. This is shown below.

In[2]:
ureg=pint.UnitRegistry()
Q_=ureg.Quantity

The given problem variables may now be defined with units.

In[3]:
T = Q_(1600, 'K')
eps_1 = 0.4
eps_2 = 0.8
eps_3 = 0
lambda_1 = Q_(2, 'um')
lambda_2 = Q_(5, 'um')

Other known quantities that will be used are shown below.

In[4]:
sigma = Q_(5.67*10**-8, 'W/(m^2*K^4)')    # Stefan-Boltzman constant #

The total, hemispherical emissivity is given by the expression shown below. $$ \varepsilon = \frac{\int_0^\infty \varepsilon_\lambda E_{\lambda, b} d \lambda}{E_b} $$

The integral in the numerator may be split into the different sections from the plot given in the problem statement. This new expression is shown below. $$ \varepsilon = \frac{\int_0^{\lambda_1} \varepsilon_1 E_{\lambda, b} d \lambda}{E_b} + \frac{\int_{\lambda_1}^{\lambda_2} \varepsilon_2 E_{\lambda, b} d \lambda}{E_b} + \frac{\int_{\lambda_2}^\infty \varepsilon_\lambda E_{\lambda, b} d \lambda}{E_b} $$

The last term in the expression above goes to zero because the emissivity in that interval is zero (this may be seen from the plot given in the problem statement). The emissivity terms in in the other two integrals are constants so they be taken out of the integral. $$ \varepsilon = \frac{\varepsilon_1 \int_0^{\lambda_1} E_{\lambda, b} d \lambda}{E_b} + \frac{\varepsilon_2 \int_{\lambda_1}^{\lambda_2} E_{\lambda, b} d \lambda}{E_b} $$

The integral terms in the expression above may readily be seen as the fractional amount of emissive power from a blackbody which is tabulated in Table 8 of Quiz 5 Formula Sheet. $$ \varepsilon = \varepsilon_1 F_{0\rightarrow \lambda_1} + \varepsilon_2 F_{\lambda_1 \rightarrow \lambda_2} $$

The above expression may be further simplified by recognizing that $ F_{\lambda_1 \rightarrow \lambda_2} $ is the difference between $ F_{0 \rightarrow \lambda_2} $ and $ F_{0 \rightarrow \lambda_1} $. This simplification is shown below. $$ \varepsilon = \varepsilon_1 F_{0\rightarrow \lambda_1} + \varepsilon_2 \left( F_{0 \rightarrow \lambda_2} - F_{0 \rightarrow \lambda_1} \right) $$

To use Table 8, the product of the temperature of wavelength are needed. The operation is shown below.

In[5]:
lamT_1 = lambda_1*T
print(lamT_1)

3200 kelvin * micrometer
In[6]:
lamT_2 = lambda_2*T
print(lamT_2)

8000 kelvin * micrometer

These values may now be used to find the $ F $ values. Interpolation is needed for the first value.

In[7]:
lamT_a = Q_(3000, 'um*K')
lamT_b = Q_(3500, 'um*K')
F_a = 0.273229
F_b = 0.382909
F_0lam1 = ((lamT_1 - lamT_a)/(lamT_b - lamT_a))*(F_b - F_a) + F_a
print(F_0lam1)

0.317101 dimensionless

The second value may be read directly from Table 8.

In[8]:
F_0lam2 = 0.856251

The total emissivity is then

In[9]:
eps = eps_1*F_0lam1 + eps_2*(F_0lam2 - F_0lam1)
print(eps)    # answer #

0.5581604 dimensionless

The total emissive power is then

In[10]:
E = eps * sigma * T**4
print(E.to('kW/m**2'))    # answer #

207.406331854848 kilowatt / meter ** 2
