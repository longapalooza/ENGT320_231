14.Example 3

Given:

A diffuse, fire brick wall of temperature $ T_s = 500 K $ has the spectral emissivity shown and is exposed to a bed of coals at $ 2000 K $.

Required:

Determine the total, hemispherical emissivity and emissive power of the fire brick wall. What is the total absorptivity of the wall to irradiation resulting from emission by the coals?

Solution:

Start first by importing any needed libraries.

In[1]:
import math
import pint # https://pint.readthedocs.io #

The unit registry may then be defined. This is shown below.

In[2]:
ureg=pint.UnitRegistry()
Q_=ureg.Quantity

The given problem variables may now be defined with units.

In[3]:
T_s = Q_(500, 'K')
T_c = Q_(2000, 'K')
eps_1 = 0.1
eps_2 = 0.5
eps_3 = 0.8
lambda_1 = Q_(1.5, 'um')
lambda_2 = Q_(10, 'um')

Other known quantities that will be used are shown below.

In[4]:
sigma = Q_(5.67*10**-8, 'W/(m^2*K^4)')    # Stefan-Boltzman constant #

The total, hemispherical emissivity of the wall is given by $$ \varepsilon \left( T_s \right) = \frac{\int_0^{\infty} \varepsilon_\lambda \left( \lambda \right) E_{\lambda, b} \left(\lambda, T_s \right) d \lambda}{E_b \left( T_s \right)} $$

The integral in the numerator may be split into different intervals. $$ \varepsilon \left( T_s \right) = \frac{\int_0^{\lambda_1} \varepsilon_1 E_{\lambda, b} \left(\lambda, T_s \right) d \lambda}{E_b \left( T_s \right)} + \frac{\int_{\lambda_1}^{\lambda_2} \varepsilon_2 E_{\lambda, b} \left(\lambda, T_s \right) d \lambda}{E_b \left( T_s \right)} + \frac{\int_{\lambda_2}^\infty \varepsilon_3 E_{\lambda, b} \left(\lambda, T_s \right) d \lambda}{E_b \left( T_s \right)} $$

For the different intervals, the emissivities are constant so they may taken out of the integrals. The remaining fractions may be seen as the $ F $ of Table 8 in Quiz 5 Formula Sheet. The above expression may be simplified to $$ \varepsilon \left( T_s \right) = \varepsilon_1 F_{0 \rightarrow \lambda_1} + \varepsilon_2 F_{\lambda_1 \rightarrow \lambda_2} + \varepsilon_3 F_{\lambda_2 \rightarrow \infty} = \varepsilon_1 F_{0 \rightarrow \lambda_1} + \varepsilon_2 \left( F_{0 \rightarrow \lambda_2} - F_{0 \rightarrow \lambda_1} \right) + \varepsilon_3 \left( F_{0 \rightarrow \infty} - F_{0 \rightarrow \lambda_2} \right) $$

To use Table 8, the product of the wavelength and temperature is used.

In[5]:
lamT_1 = lambda_1 * T_s
print(lamT_1)

750.0 kelvin * micrometer
In[6]:
lamT_2 = lambda_2 * T_s
print(lamT_2)

5000 kelvin * micrometer

Referring to Table 8, it may be seen that interpolation is needed.

In[7]:
lamT_a = Q_(600, 'um*K')
lamT_b = Q_(800, 'um*K')
F_a = 0.000000
F_b = 0.000016
F_0lam1 = ((lamT_1 - lamT_a)/(lamT_b - lamT_a))*(F_b - F_a) + F_a
print(F_0lam1)

1.2e-05 dimensionless

For $ \lambda T_2 $, no interpolation is needed.

In[8]:
F_0lam2 = 0.633726

Recalling the definition of the $ F $ term in Table 8, it may be realized that $ F_{0 \rightarrow \infty} $ is one. The total, hemispherical emissivity is then

In[9]:
eps_s = eps_1*F_0lam1 + eps_2*(F_0lam2 - F_0lam1) + eps_3*(1 - F_0lam2)
print(eps_s)    # answer #

0.6098774 dimensionless

The total emissive power is then

In[10]:
E_s = eps_s*sigma*T_s**4
print(E_s.to('kW/m**2'))    # answer #

2.1612530362499998 kilowatt / meter ** 2

The total absorptivity of the wall to radiation from the coals is $$ \alpha = \frac{\int_0^\infty \alpha_\lambda \left( \lambda \right) G_\lambda \left( \lambda \right) d \lambda}{\int_0^\infty G_\lambda \left( \lambda \right) d \lambda} $$

Since the surface is diffuse, the absorptivity is equal to the emissivity (i.e., $ \alpha_\lambda \left( \lambda \right) = \varepsilon_\lambda \left( \lambda \right) $). Moreover, since the spectral distribution of the irradiation approximates that due to emission from a blackbody at $ 2000 K $, the irradiation is proportional to the emissive power (i.e., $ G\lambda \left( \lambda \right) \propto E_{\lambda, b} \left( \lambda, T_c \right) $). The total absorptivity becomes $$ \alpha = \frac{\int_0^\infty \varepsilon_\lambda \left( \lambda \right) E_{\lambda, b} \left( \lambda, T_c \right) d \lambda}{\int_0^\infty E_{\lambda, b} \left( \lambda, T_c \right) d \lambda} $$

The integral may be split into different intervals and become $$ \alpha = \varepsilon_1 F_{0 \rightarrow \lambda_1} + \varepsilon_2 \left( F_{0 \rightarrow \lambda_2} - F_{0 \rightarrow \lambda_1} \right) + \varepsilon_3 \left( 1 - F_{0 \rightarrow \lambda_2} \right) $$

To find the $ F $ terms, the product of the wavelength and temperature are needed. This is shown below.

In[11]:
lamT_1 = lambda_1*T_c
print(lamT_1)

3000.0 kelvin * micrometer
In[12]:
lamT_2 = lambda_2*T_c
print(lamT_2)

20000 kelvin * micrometer

Table 9 may now be used to find the $ F $ terms. No interpolation is needed.

In[13]:
F_0lam1 = 0.273229
F_0lam2 = 0.985554

The total absorptivity is then

In[14]:
alpha = eps_1*F_0lam1 + eps_2*(F_0lam2 - F_0lam1) + eps_3*(1 - F_0lam2)
print(alpha)    # answer #

0.3950422
