15.Example 2¶

Given:¶

A paint baking oven consists of a long, triangular duct in which a heated surface is maintained at $1200K$ and another surface is insulated. Painted panels, which are maintained at $500K$, occupy the third surface. The triangle is of width $W=1m$ on a side, and the heated and insulated surfaces have an emissivity of $0.8$. The emissivity of the panels is $0.4$.

Required:¶

During steady-state operation, at what rate must energy be supplied to the heated side per unit length of the duct to maintain its temperature at $1200K$? What is the temperature of the insulated surface?

Solution:¶

Start first by importing any needed libraries.

In [1]:
import pint # https://pint.readthedocs.io

The unit registry may then be defined. This is shown below.

In [2]:
ureg=pint.UnitRegistry()
Q_=ureg.Quantity

The given problem variables may now be defined with units.

In [3]:
T_1=Q_(1200, 'K')
T_2=Q_(500, 'K')
W=Q_(1, 'm')
eps_1=Q_(0.8, '')
eps_R=Q_(0.8, '')
eps_2=Q_(0.4, '')

Other known quantities that will be used are shown below.

In [4]:
sigma=Q_(5.67*10**-8, 'W/(m^2*K^4)')    # Stefan-Boltzman constant #

A sketch of the system is shown below.

The equivalent radiation circuit is shown below.

Referring to Table 15.1 for a three sided enclosure, the view factor is given by $$F_{ij}=\frac{w_i+w_j-w_k}{2w_i}$$ For an equilateral triangle, all sides are equal thus the view factor becomes $$F_{ij}=\frac{W+W-W}{2W}=0.5$$

In [5]:
F_12=0.5
F_1R=0.5
F_2R=0.5

The equivalent resistance of the radiation circuit is $$R_{tot}=\frac{1-\varepsilon_1}{\varepsilon_1 A_1}+\frac{1}{A_1 F_{12} + \frac{1}{\frac{1}{A_1 F_{1R}}+\frac{1}{A_2 F_{2R}}}}+\frac{1-\varepsilon_2}{\varepsilon_2 A_2}$$ All the area terms may be expressed at the product of the width $W$ and the length $L$. This is shown below. $$R_{tot}=\frac{1-\varepsilon_1}{\varepsilon_1 W L}+\frac{1}{W L F_{12} + \frac{1}{\frac{1}{W L F_{1R}}+\frac{1}{W L F_{2R}}}}+\frac{1-\varepsilon_2}{\varepsilon_2 W L}$$ Inspecting the expression, it may be seen that there appears a length term $L$ in each denominator. The length terms $L$ may be factored out to produce $$R_{tot} L=\frac{1-\varepsilon_1}{\varepsilon_1 W}+\frac{1}{W F_{12} + \frac{1}{\frac{1}{W F_{1R}}+\frac{1}{W F_{2R}}}}+\frac{1-\varepsilon_2}{\varepsilon_2 W}$$

In [6]:
R_totL=((1-eps_1)/(eps_1*W)
        +1/(W*F_12+(1/(1/(W*F_1R)+1/(W*F_2R))))
        +(1-eps_2)/(eps_2*W))
round(R_totL.to('1/m'), 3)
Out[6]:
3.083 1/meter

The necessary heat rate to maintain the heat surface at the given temperature is given by $$q_1=\frac{E_{b1} - E_{b2}}{R_{tot}}=\frac{\sigma \left( T_1^4 - T_2^4 \right)}{R_{tot}}$$ The heat rate per unit length is given by $$q^{'}_1=\frac{q_1}{L}=\frac{\sigma \left( T_1^4 - T_2^4 \right)}{R_{tot}L}$$

In [7]:
qp_1=sigma*(T_1**4-T_2**4)/R_totL
round(qp_1.to('kW/m'), 2)    # answer #
Out[7]:
36.98 kilowatt/meter

From the circuit network, it may be seen that $$q^{'}_2=-q^{'}_1$$

In [8]:
qp_2=-qp_1
round(qp_2.to('kW/m'), 2)
Out[8]:
-36.98 kilowatt/meter

To determine the temperature of the insulated surface, radiosity at the insulated surface $J_R$ must be determined. To determine $J_R$, the radiosities at surface 1 and 2 must be determined. From the circuit network it may be seen that the radiosity at surface 1 is given by $$J_1 = E_{b1} - \frac{1-\varepsilon_1}{\varepsilon_1 W}q^{'}_1=\sigma T_1^4 - \frac{1-\varepsilon_1}{\varepsilon_1 W}q^{'}_1$$

In [9]:
J_1=sigma*T_1**4-((1-eps_1)/(eps_1*W))*qp_1
round(J_1.to('kW/m**2'), 1)
Out[9]:
108.3 kilowatt/meter2

Similarly, the radiosity at surface 2 is given by $$J_2 = E_{b2} - \frac{1-\varepsilon_2}{\varepsilon_2 W}q^{'}_2=\sigma T_2^4 - \frac{1-\varepsilon_2}{\varepsilon_2 W}q^{'}_2$$

In [10]:
J_2=sigma*T_2**4-((1-eps_2)/(eps_2*W))*qp_2
round(J_2.to('kW/m**2'), 1)
Out[10]:
59.0 kilowatt/meter2

The heat rate between surface 1 and 2 is given by $$q_{12}=\frac{J_1 - J_2}{\frac{1}{A_1 F_{12}}}$$ Expressing the area in terms of the width $W$ and length $L$ shows $$q_{12}=\frac{J_1 - J_2}{\frac{1}{W L F_{12}}}$$ Factoring out the length term $L$ shows the heat rate per unit length between surface 1 and 2 is given by $$q^{'}_{12}=\frac{q_{12}}{L}=\frac{J_1 - J_2}{\frac{1}{W F_{12}}}$$

In [11]:
qp_12=(J_1-J_2)/(1/(W*F_12))
round(qp_12.to('kW/m'), 2)
Out[11]:
24.65 kilowatt/meter

The heat rate per unit length between surface 1 and the insulated surface is then given by $$q^{'}_{1R}=q^{'}_1-q^{'}_{12}$$

In [12]:
qp_1R=qp_1-qp_12
round(qp_1R.to('kW/m'), 2)
Out[12]:
12.33 kilowatt/meter

The radiosity of the insulated surface is then $$J_R=J_1-\frac{1}{W F_{1R}}q^{'}_{1R}$$

In [13]:
J_R=J_1-(1/(W*F_1R))*qp_1R
round(J_R.to('kW/m**2'), 2)
Out[13]:
83.67 kilowatt/meter2

Since the surface is insulated, the radiosity and the emissivity are equal or $$J_R=E_{bR}=\sigma T_R^4$$ Solving for the insulated surface temperature $T_R$ shows $$T_R=\left(\frac{J_R}{\sigma}\right)^{1/4}$$

In [14]:
T_R=(J_R/sigma)**(1/4)
round(T_R, 1)    # answer #
Out[14]:
1102.2 kelvin
