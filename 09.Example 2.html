09.Example 2

Given:

Experiments have been conducted on a metallic cylinder $12.7 mm$ in diameter and $94 mm$ long. The cylinder is heated internally by an electrical heater and is subjected to a cross flow of air in a low-speed wind tunnel. Under a specific set of operating conditions for which the upstream air velocity and temperature were maintained at $V = 10 \frac{m}{s}$ and $26.2 ^\circ C$, respectively, the heater power dissipation was measured to be $P = 46 W$, while the average cylinder surface temperature was determined to be $T_s = 128.4 ^\circ C$. It is estimated that $15\%$ of the power dissipation is lost through the cumulative effect of surface radiation and conduction through the end pieces.

Required:

  1. Determine the convection heat transfer coefficient from the experimental observations.
  2. Compare the experimental result with the convection coefficient computed from an appropriate correlation.

Solution:

Start first by importing any needed libraries.

In[1]:
import math
import pint # https://pint.readthedocs.io

The unit registry may then be defined. This is shown below.

In[2]:
ureg=pint.UnitRegistry()
Q_=ureg.Quantity

The given problem variables may now be defined with units.

In[3]:
D=Q_(12.7, 'mm')
L=Q_(94, 'mm')
V=Q_(10, 'm/s')
T_inf=Q_(26.2, 'degC')
P=Q_(46, 'W')
T_s=Q_(128.4, 'degC')
f_l=Q_(15/100, '')

A drawing of the actual component being analyzed is shown below.

There will be several material properties that need to be interpolated so a Python function will be defined to perform the interpolation function. This is shown below.

In[4]:
def interp(x, x1, x2, y1, y2):
    return ((x-x1)/(x2-x1))*(y2-y1)+y1

The heat rate as given by experimental observations is
$q=\bar{h}A_s\left( T_s - T_\infty \right)$
Solving for the average convective coefficient shows
$\bar{h}=\frac{q}{A_s \left( T_s - T_\infty \right)}$
Reviewing the expression shows that the heat rate $q$ and the surface area $A_s$ must be known. The heat rate is found by the power output. Taking into account the loss through radiation and the ends of the cylinder, the heat rate is
$q=(1-f_l) P$

In[5]:
q=(1-f_l)*P
q
Out[5]:
39.1 watt

The surface area is given by
$A_s=\pi D L$

In[6]:
A_s=math.pi*D*L
round(A_s, 0)
Out[6]:
3750.0 millimeter2

The average convective coefficient is then found by
$\bar{h} = \frac{q}{A_s \left( T_s - T_\infty \right)}$

In[7]:
hb1=q/(A_s*(T_s-T_inf))
round(hb1.to('W/(m**2*K)'), 1)    # answer to part 1
Out[7]:
102.0 watt/(kelvin meter2)

The film temperature is given
$T_f=\frac{T_s+T_\infty}{2}$

In[8]:
T_f=(T_s.to('K')+T_inf.to('K'))/2
round(T_f.to('K'), 1)
Out[8]:
350.4 kelvin

Going to Table A.4 @ $T_f$ shows that interpolation is needed. This is shown below.

In[9]:
Ta=Q_(350, 'K')
Tb=Q_(400, 'K')
nua=Q_(20.92E-6, 'm**2/s')
nub=Q_(26.41E-6, 'm**2/s')
nu=interp(T_f, Ta, Tb, nua, nub)
'{:~.3E}'.format(nu)
Out[9]:
'2.097E-05 m ** 2 / s'
In[10]:
ka=Q_(30E-3, 'W/(m*K)')
kb=Q_(33.8E-3, 'W/(m*K)')
k=interp(T_f, Ta, Tb, ka, kb)
'{:~.3E}'.format(k)
Out[10]:
'3.003E-02 W / K / m'
In[11]:
Pra=0.7
Prb=0.69
Pr=interp(T_f, Ta, Tb, Pra, Prb)
round(Pr, 4)
Out[11]:
0.6999 dimensionless

The Reynolds number for flow around a cylinder is defined as
$Re_D=\frac{V D}{\nu}$

In[12]:
Re_D=V*D/nu
round(Re_D.to(''), 0)
Out[12]:
6056.0 dimensionless

Referring to Table 9.1 from the lecture notes, the following empirical relation is valid.
$\bar{Nu}_D=C Re_D^m Pr^{1/3}$
where $C=0.193$ and $m=0.618$.

In[13]:
C=0.193
m=0.618
Nub_D=C*Re_D**m*Pr**(1/3)
round(Nub_D.to(''), 2)
Out[13]:
37.27 dimensionless

Using the definition of the Nusselt number, the average convective coefficient may be found. This is shown below.
$\bar{Nu}_D=\frac{\bar{h} D}{k}$
Solving for $\bar{h}$ shows
$\bar{h}=\frac{\bar{Nu}_D k}{D}$

In[14]:
hb2=Nub_D*k/D
round(hb2.to('W/(m**2*K)'), 2)    # answer to part 2
Out[14]:
88.13 watt/(kelvin meter2)

The percent difference between the two methods is given by
$\% Diff = \Biggr\lvert \frac{\bar{h}_2 - \bar{h}_1}{\frac{\bar{h}_2+ \bar{h}_1}{2}} \Biggr\rvert$

In[15]:
pDiff=abs((hb2-hb1)/((hb2+hb1)/2))
round(pDiff, 4)
Out[15]:
0.146 dimensionless
