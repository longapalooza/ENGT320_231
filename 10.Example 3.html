10.Example 3

Given:

Hot air flows with a mass rate of $m = 0.050 \frac{kg}{s}$ through an uninsulated sheet metal duct of diameter $D = 0.15 m$, which is in the crawlspace of a house. The hot air enters at $103 ^\circ C$ and, after a distance of $L = 5 m$, cools to $85 ^\circ C$. The heat transfer coefficient between the duct outer surface and the ambient air at $T_\infty = 0 ^\circ C$ is known to be $h_o = 6 \frac{W}{m^2K}$.

Required:

  1. Calculate the heat loss $\left(W\right)$ from the duct over the length $L$.
  2. Determine the heat flux and the duct surface temperature at $x = L$.

Solution:

Start first by importing any needed libraries.

In[1]:
import math
import pint # https://pint.readthedocs.io

The unit registry may then be defined. This is shown below.

In[2]:
ureg=pint.UnitRegistry()
Q_=ureg.Quantity

The given problem variables may now be defined with units.

In[3]:
m=Q_(0.05, 'kg/s')
D=Q_(0.15, 'm')
T_m0=Q_(103, 'degC')
L=Q_(5, 'm')
T_mL=Q_(85, 'degC')
T_inf=Q_(0, 'degC')
h_o=Q_(6, 'W/(m**2*K)')

A sketch of the system is shown below.

There will be several material properties that need to be interpolated so a Python function will be defined to perform the interpolation function. This is shown below.

In[4]:
def interp(x, x1, x2, y1, y2):
    return ((x-x1)/(x2-x1))*(y2-y1)+y1

The the mean temperature $T_m$ is given by
$\bar{T}_m = \frac{T_{m,0} + T_{m,L}}{2}$

In[5]:
Tb_m=(T_m0.to('K')+T_mL.to('K'))/2
Tb_m
Out[5]:
367.15 kelvin

Table A.4 for air at $\bar{T}_m$ may now be used to obtain the various material properties needed. This is shown below.

In[6]:
Ta=Q_(350, 'K')
Tb=Q_(400, 'K')
c_pa=Q_(1.009, 'kJ/(kg*K)')
c_pb=Q_(1.014, 'kJ/(kg*K)')
c_p=interp(Tb_m, Ta, Tb, c_pa, c_pb)
round(c_p, 3)
Out[6]:
1.011 kilojoule/(kelvin kilogram)

The other properties needed will be evaluated at $T_{m,L}$. This is shown below.

In[7]:
mua=Q_(208.2E-7, 'N*s/m**2')
mub=Q_(230.1E-7, 'N*s/m**2')
mu=interp(T_mL, Ta, Tb, mua, mub)
'{:~.3E}'.format(mu)
Out[7]:
'2.118E-05 N * s / m ** 2'
In[8]:
ka=Q_(30E-3, 'W/(m*K)')
kb=Q_(33.8E-3, 'W/(m*K)')
k=interp(T_mL, Ta, Tb, ka, kb)
'{:~.3E}'.format(k)
Out[8]:
'3.062E-02 W / K / m'
In[9]:
Pra=0.7
Prb=0.69
Pr=interp(T_mL, Ta, Tb, Pra, Prb)
round(Pr, 4)
Out[9]:
0.6984 dimensionless

Now that all material properties have been found, the total heat rate may be found by
$q=m c_p \left(T_{m,L}- T_{m,0}\right)$

In[10]:
q=m*c_p*(T_mL-T_m0)
round(q.to('W'), 1)    # answer to part 1
Out[10]:
-909.6 watt

The thermal resistance circuit for the outlet is shown below.

The Reynolds number for the outlet location is given by
$Re_D=\frac{\rho u_m D}{\mu}$
However, the mean velocity at this location is not known. However, recall that the mass flow rate is given by
$m=\rho u_m A_c$
Rearranging shows
$\rho u_m = \frac{m}{A_c}$
Using this expression in the Reynolds number expression for a circular cross section shows
$Re_D=\frac{m D}{A_c \mu}=\frac{m D}{\frac{\pi}{4}D^2 \mu}=\frac{4 m}{\pi D \mu}$

In[11]:
Re_D=4*m/(math.pi*D*mu)
'{:.3E}'.format(Re_D.to(''))
Out[11]:
'2.004E+04 dimensionless'

Since the Reynolds number is greater than the critical Reynolds number for this type of flow (i.e. $Re_D \gt Re_{c,D} = 2300$), the flow is turbulent. To determine if the flow is fully develope, the length over the diameter must be greater than $10$. This is shown below.

In[12]:
round(L/D, 2)
Out[12]:
33.33 dimensionless

Since the length-diameter ratio is greater than $10$, the flow is fully developed. The appropriate empirical relation may now be selected. This is shown below.
$Nu_D=0.023 Re_D^{4/5} Pr^{0.3}$

In[13]:
Nu_D=0.023*Re_D**(4/5)*Pr**(0.3)
round(Nu_D.to(''), 2)
Out[13]:
57.08 dimensionless

The local convective coefficient may now be found from the definition of the Nusselt number. This is shown below.
$Nu_D=\frac{h D}{k}$
Solving for the local convective coefficient (the convective coefficient on the inside) shows
$h_i=\frac{Nu_D k}{D}$

In[14]:
h_i=Nu_D*k/D
round(h_i.to('W/(m**2*K)'), 2)
Out[14]:
11.65 watt/(kelvin meter2)

Referring to the thermal resistance circuit, it may be seen that the heat flux is given by
$q^{''}_s=\frac{T_{m,L}-T_\infty}{\frac{1}{h_i}+\frac{1}{h_o}}$

In[15]:
qf_s=(T_mL-T_inf)/(1/h_i+1/h_o)
round(qf_s.to('W/m**2'), 1)
Out[15]:
336.6 watt/meter2

Now that the heat flux is known, the surface temperature at the outlet may be found. Referring to the thermal resistance circuit, it may be seen that the following is true.
$q^{''}_s=\frac{T_{m,L}-T_{s,L}}{\frac{1}{h_i}}$
Rearranging shows
$T_{s,L}=T_{m,L}-\frac{q^{''}_s}{h_i}$

In[16]:
T_sL=T_mL-qf_s/h_i
round(T_sL, 1)    # answer to part 2
Out[16]:
56.1 degree_Celsius
